Dynamic Electricity

Understanding electric circuits, Ohm’s law, and current flow

Ohm’s Law

Defines the relationship between voltage, current, and resistance in an electrical circuit, which proves that current is directly proportional to voltage and inversely proportional to resistance.

This is the formula for Ohm’s Law:
V (voltage) = I (current) × R (resistance)
I (current) = V (voltage) / R (resistance)
R (resistance) = V (voltage) / I (current)

Ohm’s Law Calculator

Circuits

A closed, unbroken loop or pathway that allows electricity (electric current) to flow from a voltage source, through components, and back to the source.

Equation that links charge flow, current, and time:
Q (charge) = I (current) × t (time)

Typical Components

🔋 Battery

Provides the voltage source to drive current through the circuit.

🔌 Wires

Conductors that connect components and allow current to flow.

💡 Light Bulb

A load that converts electrical energy into light and heat.

Types of Circuits

🔀 Parallel Circuit

  • Voltage: The voltage across each component is the same (Vtotal = V1 = V2 = …)
  • Current: The total current is divided among the branches (It = I1 + I2 + …)
  • Independence: If one component breaks, the others continue to work.
  • Resistance: Total resistance is always less than the smallest individual resistor.
  • Connection: Components share the same two nodes.
  • Example: Household lighting and appliances are wired in parallel, allowing them to operate independently at full voltage.

➡️ Series Circuit

  • Voltage: The total voltage is divided across components (Vtotal = V1 + V2 + …).
  • Current: The same current flows through all components (I = I1 = I2 = …).
  • Dependence: If one component breaks, the entire circuit stops working.
  • Resistance: Total resistance is the sum of individual resistors (always greater than any single one).
  • Connection: Components connect end-to-end in a single path.
  • Example: Old holiday light strings, where one burnt bulb darkens the whole chain.

Current Direction

Electron flow direction: from negative to positive terminal (opposite of conventional current).

Kirchhoff’s Law

The total current entering a node (junction) must equal the total current leaving it, as charge is conserved.
