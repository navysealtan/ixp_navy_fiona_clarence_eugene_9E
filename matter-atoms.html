Matter & Atoms: Building Blocks of Everything

Understanding the Structure and Properties of Matter and Atoms

Mixtures

What is a Mixture?

A mixture contains 2 or more different substances mixed which are not chemically bound together and can be separated without chemical reaction.

Methods for Separating Mixtures

  1. Magnetic Attraction
  2. Evaporation
  3. Crystallization
  4. Distillation
  5. Fractional Distillation
  6. Chromatography
  7. Sedimentation
  8. Centrifugation
  9. Filtration
  10. Reverse Osmosis

Changing of Matter

🔄 Physical Change

A reversible change that does not alter the chemical composition of the substance.

Examples:

  • Melting
  • Freezing
  • Blending

⚗️ Chemical Change

An irreversible change that transforms the chemical composition of the substance.

Examples:

  • Rusty Metal
  • Paper Burning

Atoms: The Building Blocks

What is an Atom?

An atom is the smallest particle (building block) of an element that still has its own chemical properties. An atom includes:

  • Protons and Neutrons: Located in the nucleus
  • Electrons: Move freely in orbits around the nucleus

Atomic Particles and Their Charges

⊕ Proton

Charge: Positive (+)

○ Neutron

Charge: Neutral (0)

⊖ Electron

Charge: Negative (-)

Important Atomic Calculations

Mass Number: Number of protons + Number of neutrons

Mass number is usually the larger number in an atom's notation.

Atomic Number: Number of protons

Neutral Atom Rule: Amount of protons = Amount of electrons

Ions: Charged Atoms

⊕ Positive Ion

In a positive ion:

Number of Protons > Number of Electrons

⊖ Negative Ion

In a negative ion:

Number of Protons < Number of Electrons

Molecules, Elements & Compounds

🔗 Molecules

Formed when two or more atoms are joined together chemically.

🌟 Element

Contains only one type of element (made of identical atoms).

⚛️ Compound

Contains at least two different elements chemically bonded together.

The Periodic Table

📋 Period (Rows)

Represents how many electron orbits an element has.

Example: Row 6 contains elements which have 6 electron orbits.

📊 Group (Columns)

Represents how many electrons are in the outer orbit (valence electrons).

Example: Group 8 means the element has 8 electrons at the outer orbit.

Isotopes

What are Isotopes?

Isotopes are atoms that have the same amount of protons but different amounts of neutrons.

This means they are the same element but with different mass numbers.

Balancing Equations

Example: Combustion of Hydrogen

Unbalanced Equation:

H₂ + O₂ → H₂O

There are 2 H atoms on the left and 2 H on the right ✓, but oxygen is unbalanced: 2 atoms on the left, only 1 on the right ✗

✓ Balanced Equation

2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

Now all elements are equal on both sides of the equation.
