Magnetism

Exploring Magnetic Forces and Fields

What is Magnetism?

Magnetism: a physical force produced by the motion of electric charges exerted by magnets when they attract or repel each other.

Magnets are able to attract objects with magnetic materials such as iron, steel, nickel, and cobalt. Magnets cannot attract objects made from non-magnetic materials such as plastic, rubber, paper, or wood.

Types of Magnets

🧲 Permanent Magnets

Materials that can create their own magnetic fields without using external power sources. Made from magnetically hard material such as steel. Keeps its magnetism once magnetized.

🔄 Temporary Magnets

Act as a magnet only while exposed to an external magnetic field or electric current. Iron is magnetically soft and suitable. Loses magnetism easily, useful as temporary magnets or electromagnets.

Magnetic Poles

🧭 North and South Poles

The strongest part of a magnet. Two poles on opposite sides. Like poles repel, unlike poles attract.

Magnetic Field

Every magnet has a volume of space where we can detect magnetism. That volume of space is called the magnetic field. To see the magnetic field we can use iron fillings or compasses to show its shape and discover its properties.

Magnetic field lines show the shape of the magnetic field, direction of the magnetic force and strength of the magnetic field. The field lines travel from the north to the south. The magnetic field is strongest when the field lines are closer.

Creating a Uniform Magnetic Field

The magnetic field created between the north pole and south pole of two different magnets will show a series of straight field lines that are evenly spaced. Uniform magnetic field means that the strength and direction is the same everywhere.

Induced Magnetism

This picture shows induced magnetism. When the iron nails and steel paper clips touch the bar magnet, they become temporary magnets. The end touching the magnet becomes the opposite pole, and the other end becomes the opposite pole again. Because opposite poles attract, the nails and paper clips form a chain. Each piece acts like a small magnet while it is in the magnetic field.

Magnetization

Magnets can be created (magnetized) in several ways:

  1. Magnetic Induction: A magnetic material becomes magnetized when it is placed near a magnet. The magnetic field rearranges the magnetic domains inside the material.
  2. Magnetization by Stroking: A magnet is rubbed repeatedly in one direction along a magnetic material. This aligns the domains and turns the object into a magnet.
  3. Using Electricity (Electromagnet): When electric current flows through a coil of wire around an iron core, it produces a magnetic field. This forms an electromagnet, which can be turned on or off.

Demagnetization

Demagnetization is the process of removing magnetism from a magnet or magnetic material.

Ways to demagnetize a magnet:

  • Heating the magnet
  • Hammering it
  • Applying an alternating magnetic field

These actions disturb the alignment of the magnetic domains, causing the material to lose its magnetic properties.

Earth's Magnetism

🌍 Earth's Magnetic Field

The Earth acts like a giant magnet with a north pole and a south pole. A compass works because its needle is a small magnet that aligns itself with Earth's magnetic field. The north pole of the compass needle points toward Earth's north. This magnetic field is important because it helps with navigation.

Electromagnetism

When a current flows in a wire magnetic field is created around it. The field around the wire is quite weak and circular in shape.

The shape and direction of the magnetic field around the wire can be seen using iron fillings and plotting compasses. Changing the direction of current automatically affects and changes the direction of the magnetic field.

Right Hand Grip Rule

The right hand grip rule helps work out the direction of the field. The thumb shows the direction of current and the other fingers show the direction of the field.

Strength of Magnetic Field

The strength of the magnetic field around the wire (with current) can be increased by:

  1. Increasing the current in the wire.
  2. Wrapping the wire into a coil or solenoid.

The strength of the field around a solenoid can be increased by:

  1. Increasing the current in the solenoid.
  2. Increasing the number of turns of the solenoid.
  3. Wrapping the solenoid around a magnetically soft core such as iron.

When a solenoid is put around a soft iron core, it becomes an electromagnet.

Application: Electric Bell

  1. When the switch is closed, the circuit will become complete and electric current will flow.
  2. Current will flow to the solenoid on the electromagnet, causing the electromagnet to magnetize.
  3. When the electromagnet magnetizes, it will attract the soft iron armature.
  4. As the soft iron is attracted the hammer will also move causing it to hit the gong and make a bell sound.
  5. As the armature moves, there will be a gap between the soft iron and contact screw, making the circuit incomplete.
  6. When the armature moves to the gong's direction, the spring will pull the armature back to its initial position.
  7. This process will repeat over and over until the switch is manually opened.
